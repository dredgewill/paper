Growth of Indices Over Time

November 15, 2021 · Kevin Zhu

In our modern-day global economy, we would expect economies to be interlinked. One great example of interlinked markets is how the Global Financial Crisis shocked not only the American economy, but also that of Australia’s, of Europe’s… In fact, the GFC affected the entire world’s economy.

Here, I would like to demonstrate a graph over time of major indices of five economies: - the Australian ASX 200 - the Chinese CSI 300 - the European SNP 350 - the English FTSE 100 - and the American SNP 500. These indices were chosen for their varying geographic distribution. The English and European indices are expected to demonstrate a similar growth pattern given their proximity and connected economy (at least up until Brexit). The Chinese index was chosen as more of a ‘different’ factor, given the government’s tight control over the market. Their growths can be seen as follows:

It appears quite clear that there must be more to these similarities in movements than just coincidence. That huge drop in 2008 is quite obviously the GFC, but what about the other years?

Clearly there are real life events associated with those drops. Of course, similarly major events can also describe mass growth in the market. Note that since we are using indices as economic indicators, their growth rates also reflect consumer confidence, which is highly volatile. Aggregation of growth into years reduces this volatility, but it is best to keep in mind that although these indices provide a good indicator for the economy, they do not reflect the actual economy but rather the people’s impression of it.
